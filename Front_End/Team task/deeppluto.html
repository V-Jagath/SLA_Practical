The Mysterious Dwarf Planet

Pluto, once considered the ninth planet in our solar system, was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006. This distant world resides in the Kuiper Belt, a region beyond Neptune filled with icy bodies and dwarf planets.

Despite its small size, Pluto has a complex and varied surface with mountains, valleys, plains, and craters. It's known for its heart-shaped glacier, called Tombaugh Regio, which is larger than the state of Texas.

NASA's New Horizons spacecraft provided the first close-up images of Pluto in 2015, revolutionizing our understanding of this distant world. The mission revealed that Pluto is geologically active with icy volcanoes and possible subsurface oceans.

Physical Characteristics

Pluto is about two-thirds the diameter of Earth's moon and has a thin atmosphere composed mainly of nitrogen, methane, and carbon monoxide. This atmosphere freezes and collapses to the surface when Pluto is farthest from the Sun.
